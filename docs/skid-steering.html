Understanding Skid Steering

Skid steering is a technique used in vehicles without traditional steering mechanisms, such as skid-steer loaders and tracked vehicles. It allows for zero-radius turns by controlling the speed and direction of the wheels or tracks on each side of the vehicle.

This maneuverability is achieved through differential speed control, where the wheels or tracks on one side move at different speeds or in opposite directions compared to the other side.

Mathematics of Skid Steering

The control of skid steering involves calculating the velocities of the left and right sides of the vehicle using linear and angular velocities. The equations are as follows:

\[ v_L = v - \frac{\omega \times w}{2} \]
\[ v_R = v + \frac{\omega \times w}{2} \]

Where:

  • \(v_L\) is the velocity of the left side.
  • \(v_R\) is the velocity of the right side.
  • \(v\) is the desired linear velocity of the vehicle.
  • \(\omega\) is the desired angular velocity.
  • \(w\) is the width of the vehicle's wheelbase.

The turning radius \(R\) of the vehicle can be calculated using the formula:

\[ R = \frac{v}{\omega} \]

This approach allows for precise control of the vehicle's motion, especially in environments requiring tight maneuverability. By adjusting the velocities, skid-steer vehicles can perform tasks efficiently without the need for a traditional steering mechanism.
